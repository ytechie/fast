The science behind the stages

A plain-English walkthrough of what the app is modeling, why those time windows were chosen, and links to the research each claim is based on.

Not medical advice. This page is for educational and personal-curiosity purposes only. Everyone's metabolism is different — the times below are population-level estimates from research, not predictions for any individual. Fasting can be risky for people with diabetes, eating disorders, or who are pregnant, breastfeeding, or on certain medications. Talk to a clinician before changing how you eat.

The big idea: the "metabolic switch"

Intermittent fasting research is built around a concept called the metabolic switch — the transition from running primarily on glucose (sugar) and liver glycogen to running primarily on fat and ketone bodies. The depletion of liver glycogen during a fast triggers fat mobilization and ketogenesis, and most of the claimed benefits of time-restricted eating come from spending time on the fat-burning side of that switch.

Primary review: de Cabo R, Mattson MP. Effects of Intermittent Fasting on Health, Aging, and Disease. N Engl J Med. 2019;381(26):2541–2551.

The five stages, with citations

🍽️ Fed / Anabolic 0–4h

After a meal, blood glucose and insulin rise. Insulin's job is to move glucose into cells and shut down fat burning while incoming fuel is being processed. Excess carbohydrate is stored as glycogen in the liver (~70–100g) and skeletal muscle; excess energy beyond that is stored as fat.

The 4-hour boundary is approximate — bigger or carb-heavy meals keep insulin elevated longer, which is why the "carby meal" tag shifts the timeline.

Tabular reference for postprandial insulin/glucose response and glycogen storage: Endotext: Physiology of Insulin Secretion · Frayn KN, Metabolic Regulation: A Human Perspective (Wiley) — the standard text for this material.

⏳ Early Fasting 4–12h

Once digestion winds down, insulin falls. The body draws on circulating glucose first, then begins releasing glucose from liver glycogen to keep blood sugar steady. Gluconeogenesis (making glucose from non-carb sources like amino acids and glycerol) starts ramping up.

By the end of an overnight fast (~12h), liver glycogen is typically about 50% depleted — which is why the next stage starts around then.

Rothman DL, Magnusson I, Katz LD, Shulman RG, Shulman GI. Quantitation of hepatic glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis in fasting humans with 13C NMR. Science. 1991;254(5031):573–576. Direct measurement of glycogen decline and rising gluconeogenesis through the first ~22h.

🔄 Metabolic Switch 12–18h

This is the "switch" itself. Liver glycogen drops to ~30% or lower, lipolysis (fat breakdown) accelerates, and the liver begins producing low levels of ketone bodies. Norepinephrine rises slightly — which is why fasted people often report a calmer, more alert kind of focus rather than the foggy crash you might expect.

The exact timing varies a lot by person, prior diet, and activity level. People already adapted to lower-carb eating cross this earlier; people coming off a high-carb day cross it later.

Zauner C, Schneeweiss B, Kranz A, et al. Resting energy expenditure in short-term starvation is increased as a result of an increase in serum norepinephrine. Am J Clin Nutr. 2000;71(6):1511–1515. Documents the norepinephrine rise (and the surprising increase in metabolic rate, not decrease) during a multi-day fast.
Also: Anton SD, Moehl K, Donahoo WT, et al. Flipping the Metabolic Switch: Understanding and Applying the Health Benefits of Fasting. Obesity. 2018;26(2):254–268.

🧠 Ketogenesis Rising 18–24h

With glycogen mostly gone, the liver shifts production toward ketone bodies — primarily beta-hydroxybutyrate (BHB) and acetoacetate. Many people cross BHB > 0.5 mmol/L in this window. That number is the widely-cited threshold for nutritional ketosis, the point at which the brain starts meaningfully using ketones alongside glucose.

0.5 mmol/L is a convention, not a magic number — it's the cutoff popularized by Phinney and Volek that's become the de-facto definition in research and consumer devices. Individual blood levels at 18–24h range from ~0.2 to well over 1.0 mmol/L.

Phinney SD, Volek JS. The Art and Science of Low Carbohydrate Living. Beyond Obesity LLC; 2011. (Origin of the 0.5 mmol/L "nutritional ketosis" threshold.)
Cahill GF Jr. Fuel metabolism in starvation. Annu Rev Nutr. 2006;26:1–22. The classic paper on the timeline of ketone rise and the brain's switch to ketones as a fuel source.

🔥 Sustained Ketosis 24h+

By 24 hours, liver glycogen is essentially gone. The body runs primarily on fat and ketones; BHB typically continues climbing toward 1–2 mmol/L over the next day. Growth hormone secretion rises sharply during this window — studies have measured multi-fold increases in GH pulse amplitude after 24 hours without food.

Autophagy caveat: autophagy (cellular "self-cleaning") is the most over-hyped fasting benefit. Robust measurements in humans mostly come from fasts of ~48h or longer. At 24h there's plausibly some increase in autophagy markers, but most of the dramatic "autophagy starts at 16/18/24 hours" claims you'll see online are extrapolated from rodent studies. Be skeptical.

Hartman ML, Veldhuis JD, Johnson ML, et al. Augmented growth hormone (GH) secretory burst frequency and amplitude, increased GH half-life, and enhanced 24-hour GH production rates in two-day fasted normal men. J Clin Endocrinol Metab. 1992;74(4):757–765.
Bagherniya M, Butler AE, Barreto GE, Sahebkar A. The effect of fasting or calorie restriction on autophagy induction: A review of the literature. Ageing Res Rev. 2018;47:183–197. Good summary of what we can and can't say about autophagy in humans.

The tag math, explained

The "Tag this fast" controls shift the stage timeline by a fixed number of hours. These offsets are rough estimates chosen to be directionally correct, not precise predictions. The actual effect on any individual will depend on body composition, activity, prior meals, and many other factors.

🥗 Light, low-carb meal (−2h)

A meal that's mostly protein and fat with minimal carbohydrate keeps insulin lower and leaves less glycogen to deplete, so the metabolic switch arrives earlier. A −2h offset corresponds to the faster ketone rise consistently seen in low-carb-adapted subjects.

🍕 Big or carby meal (+2.5h) · 🍦 Sugar / dessert (+3.5h)

High-carb or high-sugar meals top up liver glycogen and keep insulin elevated longer. The body burns through that fuel first before reaching for fat, which pushes the entire stage timeline later by several hours. The bigger the carb/sugar load, the bigger the delay.

🚶 Walked 30+ min (−1.5h) · 💪 Workout (−2.5h)

Physical activity, especially anything that taxes muscle glycogen, drains your carbohydrate stores faster. With less glycogen left to burn through, the switch to fat and ketones happens earlier. This is one of the better-supported tag offsets in the app.

Volek JS, Freidenreich DJ, Saenz C, et al. Metabolic characteristics of keto-adapted ultra-endurance runners. Metabolism. 2016;65(3):100–110. Shows how exercise interacts with fat-adaptation and ketone availability.

🍷 Alcohol (+2h)

This one is interesting and counter-intuitive. Even zero-carb alcohol (spirits, dry wine) pauses ketogenesis. The liver metabolizes ethanol via reactions that consume NAD+ and pile up NADH. Both fat oxidation (β-oxidation) and ketone production need NAD+ as a cofactor — so while the liver is busy clearing ethanol, those pathways stall. The +2h offset reflects roughly how long ketogenesis is suppressed per typical drink.

Lieber CS. Alcoholic fatty liver: its pathogenesis and mechanism of progression to inflammation and fibrosis. Alcohol. 2004;34(1):9–19. Covers the NAD+/NADH shift and its effect on hepatic lipid metabolism.

🍩 Cheat day (no timeline impact)

Cheat day is a record-keeping tag only — it doesn't shift the stages because if you weren't really doing OMAD, the model's assumptions don't apply. It's there so you can spot patterns in your own data later.

Things this app is intentionally not modeling

  • Calories. OMAD's benefits come partly from the eating window and partly from natural calorie reduction. The app tracks time, not food.
  • Macronutrient ratios. Two "big carby" meals can have very different effects depending on fiber, fat content, and order of eating. The tag is a rough proxy.
  • Personal adaptation. Someone who's been doing OMAD for 6 months will cross every stage earlier than someone on day 3. The app uses population-level averages.
  • Blood ketone or glucose measurement. If you want a real number for your own body, a CGM or a blood ketone meter is the only way.
